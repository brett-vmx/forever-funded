# Lesson 1 — The Stakes

*Forever Funded course — recording script*

This is the foundation lesson — more heart and vision than tactics, so it's
mostly you to camera. It's also the lesson most likely to be shown publicly (on
the course outline page), so it has to open on *care*, not on a label. Everything
practical in the course rests on the student believing what this lesson argues.

**Promise to open with:** "By the end of this lesson, you won't think of your
supporter email as a chore you owe people. You'll see it for what it actually is —
and once you see it, you can't unsee it."

---

## The core idea (say this plainly, early)

For a lot of the people who support you, this email — the one you're about to
write — *is* the relationship. Not part of it. The whole thing.

Think about who's on your list. Many of them aren't in your city. Your lives don't
overlap at school pickup or church or the grocery store. If you're overseas, you
might see them once every two, three, four years. So the update isn't *one* of the
ways they stay connected to you and the work — for a lot of them, it's the *only*
way. That's the stakes. When an update gets skimmed and deleted, it isn't a small
miss. It's a relationship quietly going thin.

That reframes everything we're about to do. This isn't about marketing tricks.
It's about stewarding the one thread some of these people have to you.

---

## The argument (the teachable core)

### 1. The email is the relationship
Lead here, because it's the part almost nobody says out loud. The supporter who
prays for you faithfully, who has given for years, who tears up when your name
comes up at church — if the only contact they have is your email, then your email
is carrying the entire weight of that relationship. Write like that's true,
because it is.

### 2. People are watching — and I'll go first
*[Be honest and self-implicating here. This is what earns you the right to be
blunt.]*
I'll confess something. I get a lot of missionary updates, and I take mental notes
on every one — I can't help it. And here's the uncomfortable part: I catch myself
skipping someone's prayer requests every single time, not because I don't care,
but because the font is so small it's a pain to read. I feel guilty, and I still
do it. I've also *sent* updates I know got skimmed. So I'm not pointing at you.
I'm pointing at all of us.

Because here's the reality: people are watching. They form an impression of your
ministry from your emails the same way they form one of any organization — whether
or not that feels fair. And engagement is a spectrum. Your mom reads every word
you'll ever write. The supporter on the margin decides in about two seconds. You
are not writing for your mom. You're writing for the person who'll quietly drift
if you give them a reason to — and "the font was too small" or "the subject line
was just *Simpson Update: April 2026*" is a real enough reason.

### 3. So steward it like the marketing department
Now I'll say the thing that might make you flinch: you are the marketing
department for your ministry. Stay with me, because this is good news, not a
compromise.

Marketers obsess over the subject line because they know it's half the battle.
YouTubers sweat the thumbnail and title because they know nobody watches what they
don't click. Every brand on earth is trying to turn a stranger into a
relationship. And here's the thing — you have the most worth-it thing on the other
side of that door that anyone could have. A brand wants a customer. You want a
partner in prayer for eternal work. So you shouldn't care *less* about earning
attention than a marketer does. You should care *more*. Stewarding someone's
attention well isn't selling yourself. It's honoring the person and inviting them
into something that matters.

### 4. Story carries; numbers confirm *(a plant for Lesson 2)*
One more, briefly. The best nonprofits prove their impact with a mix of story and
numbers — but the *story* is what people remember, and the numbers are what make
them trust it. Most missionary updates do the opposite: they lead with stats and
bury the story, or skip the story entirely. We're going to flip that, starting
next lesson.

## The proof — my own list

*[This is the emotional center. Tell it slowly and humbly. Two versions below —
use the fuller one in the paid lesson, the lighter one anywhere public.]*

### Fuller version (paid lesson)

I want to tell you what this has meant in my own ministry, and I want to be
careful how I say it, because I don't believe for a second that this is about my
tactics or my design skills. I believe it's a partnership — God's work through the
prayers of faithful people, alongside our effort to communicate well. With that
said:

Investing in our email list, year after year, has had a natural fruit. Our
year-end giving has grown — this past year it was around thirty-five thousand
dollars in extra gifts. But it's the hard seasons that showed me what these
relationships really were. When we had to evacuate China overnight, the people on
our list gave an extra fifteen thousand dollars to help us through the transition.
When I had my first cancer surgery, they gave seventeen thousand. And when the
cancer came back and I went through brachytherapy, chemo, and radiation, they gave
— I still can't quite believe it — about seventy thousand dollars, when the actual
costs were closer to thirty-five or forty. I told them, plainly, when we'd hit the
need. And they kept giving anyway.

I'm not telling you that to boast. I'm telling you because those gifts weren't
responses to clever emails. They were responses to a *relationship* — one that,
across an ocean and through cancer, stayed alive almost entirely through the
updates we sent. That's what's at stake when you sit down to write. Not a
newsletter. A lifeline.

### Lighter version (public outline / website)

Over the years, the people on our email list have carried us through things I
never could have planned for — an overnight evacuation from China, multiple cancer
surgeries, and more. And I'm convinced it wasn't because of clever emails. It was
because those emails kept a real relationship alive across an ocean, year after
year, when almost nothing else could. That's what this course is really about:
not better newsletters, but relationships strong enough to hold.

---

## Assignment

Before you write another word, do this:

1. Open the last five missionary or ministry updates *you* received. Notice which
   you read to the end, which you skimmed, which you deleted — and be honest about
   *why*.
2. Now open your own last two or three updates and read them as if you were a busy
   supporter who likes you but is in a hurry. Would you open them? Read to the end?
   Feel closer to the sender by the last line?

You don't have to fix anything yet. Just see it. The rest of the course is how you
fix it.

---

## Self-check (this one's for your heart, not the Coach)

- Am I writing for my mom, or for the supporter on the margin?
- If this email were the *only* window a supporter had into our ministry, would it
  be enough?
- Would I open this and read it myself?
- Does this invite people in — or just report at them?

Unlike the lessons ahead, the Coach can't check most of this. It's the posture
everything else is built on. Get this right, and the rest of the course is just
learning the craft to match the care.

---

## Recording notes (for you)

- **Mostly talking head.** This is vision and heart; your presence and your story
  carry it far more than any slide. Slow down, especially for the list story.
- **Two versions of the giving story.** Record the fuller one for the paid lesson;
  use the lighter one (or a cut of it) anywhere a stranger might see it. Keep the
  surplus detail out of the public version.
- **Go first.** Confess that *you* skim, that *you've* sent forgettable updates,
  before you describe the pattern in others. Self-implication is what lets you be
  blunt without stinging the very people you're teaching.
- **This may be public.** Record it knowing a stranger on your website might watch
  the open — so lead with care, and let the "marketing department" reframe arrive
  only after you've earned it.
- **Pair it with Lesson 2.** "The Stakes" sets up *why*; "You're a Storyteller"
  starts the *how.* Tee that up at the end.
