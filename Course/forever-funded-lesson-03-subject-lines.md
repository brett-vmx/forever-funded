# Lesson 3 — Subject Lines

*Forever Funded course — recording script*

## Open — to camera

*[Talking head.]*

In the last lesson we did the hard work: we found one story and told it well. In
this lesson we're going to make sure somebody actually reads it.

Here's the uncomfortable truth about your supporter letters. You can write the
most moving update of your life, and most of your supporters will decide whether
to read it based on about six words they see in their inbox — your subject line.
It's the single most-read thing you write and, for most of us, the least
thought-about. We pour an hour into the letter and then type "October Update" in
two seconds on our way out the door.

By the end of this lesson, that stops. You'll know how to write a subject line
that earns the open — and the good news is you've already done most of the work,
because the best subject line you'll ever write is already sitting inside the
letter you wrote last lesson.

---

## The core idea

*[Screen: put one line of text up — "October Update" — then beside it "She came
back at 7 in the morning."]*

Look at these two. Same letter underneath both. One of them sounds like homework.
The other one makes you need to know what happened.

That's the whole game. A subject line isn't a label you stick on the outside of
the envelope. It's a promise of a story — and a little bit of curiosity that the
letter then pays off. The second one isn't a trick. It's just the truest, most
specific moment of the story, moved to the front.

So we're not learning marketing here. We're learning to take the best thing
inside your letter and let people see it before they decide to open.

---

## Five ways to write one that gets opened

*[You can keep these as on-screen titles as you go.]*

### 1. Mine the story for it
Don't invent a subject line out of thin air. Go back into the letter you already
wrote and find the most vivid, specific moment — the quote, the object, the
turning point — and start there. If you told the story well in Lesson 2, your
subject line is already in there waiting for you.

### 2. Be specific, not categorical
"Ministry Update" could be the subject of anyone's letter, anywhere, ever.
That's exactly the problem — it tells the reader nothing, so it earns nothing.
"The first baptism in this village in a decade" could only be *your* letter.
Specific is what creates curiosity. The more particular, the more openable.

### 3. Open a small loop
The best subject lines leave a question hanging that only the letter can answer.
"We almost gave up on the building. Then a stranger called." You can feel
yourself wanting to know what the stranger said. That pull is honest — there's a
real story that answers it.

### 4. Keep your promise
*[Say this one slowly — it matters for this audience.]*
Now, a caution. Curiosity is only fair if the letter delivers on it. Don't
promise drama you don't have. Don't write "You won't believe what happened" over
an ordinary update — that's clickbait, and supporters smell it, and it costs you
the one thing you can't afford to lose, which is their trust. Everything we teach
in this course is built on trust. A great subject line is just the truest moment
of a true story. Honest curiosity, never bait.

### 5. Write it last, and ask for help
Here's the workflow that makes this easy: write the whole letter first, *then*
harvest the subject line from it. Trying to write the subject line on a blank
page is hard; pulling it out of a finished story is simple. And when you've got a
few options, say them out loud to your spouse or a friend and ask one question —
"which of these would you actually open?" You'll be surprised how fast the right
one becomes obvious.

Coach does for you — when you send it a draft, it pulls two or three stronger
subject lines straight out of your own letter, so you've always got options to

## Teardown — on screen

*[Screen-share. Put the BEFORE column up, read each one flat and bored, then
reveal the AFTER and explain why it works.]*

| Before (dead on arrival) | After (earns the open) |
|---|---|
| October Newsletter | She came back at 7 in the morning |
| Ministry Update | The whole village heard the first baptism in a decade |
| Prayer Requests | We almost gave up. Then a stranger called. |
| Thank You & Updates | What the new well changed by Tuesday afternoon |

Walk through it: every "after" is *specific*, every one *comes from a real
moment* in the letter, and every one *opens a small loop* your reader wants
closed. None of them oversell. They just move the best true thing to the front.

*[Then the live demo — this is the most valuable minute of the lesson.]*

Let me show you something. Take one letter — the Mariam story from last lesson —
and watch how many honest subject lines live inside one good story:

- She came back at 7 in the morning
- "Now I have forty questions"
- The Gospel of Luke, full of her handwriting
- I thought I'd lost her
- The conversation that lasted until noon

Five subject lines, one story, all true. *[Pick one and say why.]* I'd probably
lead with "She came back at 7 in the morning," because it sets a scene and opens
a loop without giving anything away. But there's no single right answer — the
point is that a good story is generous. Write several, read them aloud, pick the
one you'd open.

## What to leave behind

*[Quick screen list.]*

These tell your reader "you can safely skip this," so retire them:

- "January Update," "October Newsletter," "Newsletter #47"
- "Prayer Letter" / "Support Update" / your org's name plus the month
- Anything that could be the subject of literally anyone's letter

If you could paste your subject line onto a stranger's letter and it would still
fit, it's too generic. Make it so specific it could only be yours.

## The second line nobody uses — preview text

*[Screen: show an inbox row — sender name, the subject line, then the grayed-out
second line beneath it. Point right at that second line.]*

Before we leave the inbox, look at this line right here. Underneath your subject
line, most inboxes show a second line of text. It's called preview text — or
preheader text — and here's the thing: almost nobody sets it. So what shows up
instead? Usually whatever your email happens to start with — "View this email in
your browser," or "Dear friends and family, we hope this finds you well." You've
got a second free line to pull someone in, and it's being spent on boilerplate.

Every email tool gives you this — Mailchimp, Kit, all of them — usually a little
box labeled "preview text" or "preheader." Most missionaries don't know it's
there, or figure it's not worth the thirty seconds. It is.

The trick is simple: don't *repeat* your subject line — *extend* it. Your subject
line opens a loop; your preview text widens it a little.

*[Screen: before/after, using the Mariam subject from earlier.]*

- **Subject:** She came back at 7 in the morning
- **Preview (wasted):** View this email in your browser
- **Preview (better):** Three months after I was sure I'd pushed her too hard.

Feel how the second one keeps pulling? The subject makes you curious; the preview
makes you a little *more* curious, without giving away the ending. Thirty seconds,
one more reason to open.

And if you forget — the Forever Funded Email Coach will catch it. If your preview
text is missing or stuck on filler, it'll flag it and hand you a few options
pulled straight from your letter.

## Assignment

Go back to the letter you wrote for Lesson 2. Don't change the letter — just:

1. Write **five** subject lines for it, pulled from the actual story.
2. Read all five aloud to one other person.
3. Ask them: "Which of these would you open?"
4. Use that one.
5. Then write one line of **preview text** for it — extend the subject, don't
   repeat it — and set it in your email tool.

## Self-check *(this is exactly what the Coach checks)*

- Would a busy supporter actually open this?
- Is it specific — or could it be anyone's letter?
- Does it come from the real story inside?
- Does the letter keep the promise the subject line makes?
- Did I set preview text that adds to the subject, instead of leaving it on filler?
