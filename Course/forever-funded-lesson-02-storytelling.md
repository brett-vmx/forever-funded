# Lesson 2 — You're a Storyteller

*Forever Funded course — recording-ready outline*

This is the most important lesson in the course, so it should feel like the
strongest one. The value here is your examples and your judgment, not slides.
Plan to spend most of the runtime inside real before/after letters, marking them
up on screen while you talk.

**Promise to open with:** "By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to write an
update that a supporter reads to the end — and remembers next month. Not because
you reported more, but because you told one thing well."

---

## The core idea (say this plainly, early)

A supporter can't hold ten updates in their head. They can hold one story.
One story, told with detail and a little vulnerability, does more for connection,
prayer, and giving than a full page of accomplishments. Your monthly instinct is
to *report*. This lesson is about replacing the report with a story.

> The reflex to fight all the way through: a newsletter is not an activity log.

---

## The seven moves (the teachable core)

Give each one a name on screen — names make them repeatable.

### 1. One Story, One Letter
Pick a single moment from the last few weeks. Not the month in review — one
scene. Everything else either supports that story or gets cut. If two stories are
fighting for the lead, save one for next time (that's not waste — it's your next
letter half-written).

### 2. Start in the Scene
Cut the runway. Most letters open with "We hope this finds you well, it's been a
busy season…" — that's throat-clearing, and it's where people stop reading. Open
*inside* the moment instead. Drop the reader into the kitchen, the clinic, the
classroom on the day it happened.

### 3. Show the Change
A story is just: someone (or something) was one way, then a moment happened, and
now it's different. You don't need a dramatic arc — you need a *before* and an
*after*. If nothing changed, it's an announcement, not a story.

### 4. Let Someone Else Talk
One real quote is worth a paragraph of your summary. What did the person actually
say? Write it the way they said it. A single line of dialogue makes the whole
letter feel true.

### 5. Earn Trust with the Telling Detail
"We saw God move" tells the reader nothing. "The Gospel of Luke, its margins full
of her handwriting" tells them everything — and notice *how much*: you never said
she studied for weeks or took it seriously, but the reader pictures all of it from
one image. That's the skill. Not *more* detail — the *right* detail. Two things
make a detail earn its place:

**Concrete, not decorative.** Reach for specific nouns and numbers, not mood
adjectives. "She showed up at 7am, before I'd made coffee" beats "her weathered,
hopeful face as she stood trembling at the door." The first is faster *and* more
vivid, because the reader builds the picture themselves.

**One, not all.** A novelist describes the whole room — they've got 300 pages and
a reader who wants to linger. You've got an email and a reader racing to inbox
zero. So don't describe everything: pick the single object or moment that makes
the reader build the rest, and trust them to do it.

The line to remember: *be lavish with one detail, ruthless with everything else.*
(That second half is Lesson 4 — this is the point where storytelling and tight
writing turn out to be the same skill.)

(Boundaries note: when safety or privacy requires it, change names and blur the
location, but keep the *texture*. Anonymized and specific beats vague and safe.)

### 6. Be a Person, Not a Report
Let them see you, not just the ministry. The doubt before the breakthrough. The
thing you got wrong. Supporters stay for decades because they're connected to a
*person*, and connection requires a little vulnerability. Model it here.

### 7. Keep the Thread
Follow up on stories from earlier letters. "Remember Mariam, who I wrote about in
the spring?" turns a series of updates into a relationship — supporters start
waiting for the next installment the way they'd wait for the next episode.

## Teardown segment (the heart of the lesson — show this on screen)

Put the BEFORE on screen, read it aloud the way a supporter would skim it on
their phone, then rebuild it live.

### BEFORE — the ministry report (what most missionaries send)

> Dear friends and family,
>
> We hope this update finds you well! It's been a busy and fruitful season here.
> This month we held three Bible studies, participated in a community outreach
> event, and continued our language studies. We also met with several local
> pastors about future partnership opportunities. Please keep praying for our
> ministry as we seek to make disciples, and for our ongoing support needs.
> Praise God for all He is doing!

Mark up on screen what's wrong: no story, buried under a list, no people, no
quote, generic praise, an ask with no warmth behind it. A supporter skims it and
archives it.

### AFTER — one story, told well

> **Subject: She came back at 7 in the morning**
>
> Mariam almost didn't come back.
>
> After our first conversation about Jesus this spring, she went quiet. Stopped
> answering my messages. I assumed I'd pushed too hard and lost her.
>
> Then last Tuesday she was at our door at 7am, before I'd even made coffee,
> holding the little Gospel of Luke I'd given her — its margins crammed with her
> handwriting.
>
> "I read all of it," she said. "Twice. Now I have forty questions."
>
> We sat at the kitchen table until noon.

Then narrate *why* it works, pointing at each line: starts in the scene, one
story, a clear before/after, a real quote, specifics (7am, the margins, forty
questions), and you let yourself look human ("I assumed I'd lost her"). Note the
subject line came straight out of the story — which is exactly the bridge into
Lesson 3.

*(Optional second teardown: take a letter that's all numbers — "120 attended,
14 decisions, 3 baptisms" — and show how to keep one number that matters and
turn the rest into a single face. This sets up the "where numbers belong"
point below.)*

---

## What to leave out (and where the leftovers go)

- The activity log. Pick one; the rest can wait for future letters.
- Stacked statistics. One number a reader can feel ("the first baptism in this
  village in a decade") beats five they'll forget.
- Insider summary ("the Lord did much"). Show the much; don't label it.

Numbers aren't banned — they're seasoning. One, in service of the story, lands.
A pile of them turns a letter back into a report.

---

## Assignment (give them something to actually do)

Write one letter, this week, about a single moment from the last month.
Rules:
1. Open inside the scene — no "we hope this finds you well."
2. Include one real quote.
3. Show one thing that changed.
4. Cut everything that isn't part of that story.

Then read it aloud before sending. If you stumble or get bored reading your own
letter, your supporters will too.

---

## Self-check (mirrors the Email Coach — point this out)

- Is there *one* story, or several competing for the lead?
- Did I bury it under a greeting or a list?
- Is there a real quote, and one concrete, telling detail (not stacked description)?
- Did I let myself be a little human?
- Did I follow up on anything from a past letter?

Tell them: this is exactly what the Email Coach checks, so the habit they build
here is the habit the tool reinforces later.

## Recording notes (for you)

- **Talking-head intro (short):** deliver the promise and the core idea to
  camera — this is the part that benefits most from your voice and presence.
- **Screen-share for the teardowns:** this is the bulk of the lesson. Have the
  BEFORE and AFTER in a doc and mark them up live; your narration matters more
  than the production.
- **Keep segments short** and record the seven moves as separate clips — easier
  to re-record any single one without redoing the lesson.
- **Use your own real letters** wherever you can (anonymized). Your actual
  examples are the moat; invented ones are fine as backup.
