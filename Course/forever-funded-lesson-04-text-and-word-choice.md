# Lesson 4 — Text & Word Choice

*Forever Funded course — recording script*

Same format as before: plain text is what you say; *italics in brackets* are
stage directions. The teardown is again the center of gravity — most of the value
is rewriting a real paragraph on screen.

---

## Open — to camera

*[Talking head.]*

So far we've gotten people to open your email (subject line) and we've given them
one good story to read (storytelling). This lesson is about the words themselves —
making sure that once someone starts reading, nothing trips them up or wears them
out.

There are really only two things that make a supporter quietly give up on a
letter: words they don't understand, and more words than they needed. That's it.
This lesson kills both — without stripping the life out of your writing, which is
the part most people get wrong.

---

## The core idea

You live inside a world of ministry words. Your supporters don't. Every time they
hit a term they don't know, or a sentence that rambles, they drift a little
further toward the archive button. Your job is to sound like a real person
talking to a friend — plainly, and only as long as the story needs.

One caution up front, because it's the trap: tighter is not the same as shorter.
We're not turning your letters into telegrams. A vivid story that takes a few
extra lines beats a one-sentence summary every time. The goal is to cut what
doesn't serve the story, and protect what does.

---

## The six moves

### 1. Talk, don't announce
Read your sentence and ask: would I ever *say* this out loud to a friend? "We are
praising God for the fruit we are seeing" is announcing. "Two guys I've been
meeting with decided to follow Jesus" is talking. Write the way you talk.

### 2. Kill the jargon
*[Screen: put up a list of common ministry words.]*
Every world has insider words, and ministry has a lot of them: "UPG," "the
field," "leverage," "intercession," "kingdom impact," "covet your prayers,"
"reached," "mobilize." Your supporters didn't go to your training. If a word
would make sense to your team but not to your aunt, cut it or swap it for plain
English.

### 3. Keep it — but open the door
Sometimes a term is worth keeping, especially one that's central to your work. The
move isn't to delete it — it's to bring the reader along with a quick definition.
"Creation to Christ stories" means nothing to most people. "Creation to Christ
stories — simple Bible stories told out loud, for people who can't read" means
everything. Keep the term; open the door.

### 4. Tighter, not shorter
*[Say this slowly — it's the one people get wrong.]*
Now tighten. Cut the filler words, and give each paragraph one job — if a
paragraph is holding two different ideas, split it. But hear me: tightening is
about cutting what's *limp*, not what's *vivid*. Watch the difference:

*[Screen: two versions.]*
- Over-tightened: "A woman read Luke and had questions."
- Right: "She showed up at 7 in the morning holding the Gospel of Luke, its
  margins full of her handwriting. 'I have forty questions.'"

The second one is longer — and it's better, because every extra word is doing
work. That's the line. Cut the padding; keep the picture.

### 5. Be careful without being cryptic
If you work somewhere sensitive, you'll need to be vague about some things — and
that's right. Just make sure "careful" doesn't turn into "confusing." A reader
should never be puzzled by your discretion. If a sentence reads like a secret
code, smooth it out so it sounds natural while still protecting what it needs to.

### 6. Catch the small stuff
Then proofread. Typos and grammar slips don't just look sloppy — they make people
quietly assume you didn't care enough to check. Read the whole thing out loud;
your ear catches what your eye skips right over. Do this *before* you send it to
your spouse, a friend, or the Coach — respect their time by handing them clean
copy, so they can focus on the big stuff instead of hunting for a missing period.

## Teardown — on screen

*[Screen-share. Put the BEFORE up, read it aloud in a flat "newsletter voice,"
then rewrite it live.]*

### BEFORE — jargon and bloat

> We are praising God for the fruit we are seeing in our UPG engagement. After a
> season of significant intentionality around relational evangelism and
> contextualized discipleship pathways, the Lord has moved powerfully. We would
> covet your continued partnership and intercession as we seek to leverage every
> open door for kingdom impact in the days ahead.

Mark it up: "UPG," "intentionality around relational evangelism,"
"contextualized discipleship pathways," "covet your intercession," "leverage,"
"kingdom impact," "season" — almost every line has a word your supporter has to
translate, and underneath all of it, there's no actual story. It's fog.

### AFTER — plain, tight, and still warm

> Two men we've been having coffee with every week decided to follow Jesus.
> We're praying about how to help them grow — would you pray with us?

Now narrate why it's better: the jargon is gone, the abstractions ("fruit,"
"open doors") became real people having real coffee, it's a third the length —
*and* it's warmer, with a clear, simple ask. Shorter happened on its own once the
fog cleared. We didn't aim for short; we aimed for clear and true, and short came
along for the ride.

---

## Assignment

Take the letter you've been working on since Lesson 2. Three quick passes:

1. **Jargon hunt.** Circle every word your non-ministry friend wouldn't know. Cut
   it or define it in a few words.
2. **Tightening pass.** Cut filler. Split any paragraph that's holding two ideas.
   Leave the vivid detail alone.
3. **Read it aloud.** Fix every place you stumble, and catch the typos — before
   anyone else sees it.

---

## Self-check *(this is what the Coach checks — except the read-aloud, which is on you)*

- Does this sound like me talking to a friend?
- Would someone outside my ministry understand every term?
- Did I define anything I chose to keep?
- Is it as short and clear as it can be — *without* losing the warmth and detail?
- Where I had to be careful, does it still read clearly?
- Did I read it aloud and catch the typos?

## Recording notes (for you)

- **Talking head for the core idea and the "tighter, not shorter" caution** — the
  nuance lands better as you, and it's the part students most need to hear from a
  real voice, not a rule.
- **Screen-share for the teardown** — marking up the jargon and rewriting live is
  the whole lesson. Your real edits are more convincing than any rule.
- **Use your own before/after** if you have one — an old letter of yours, full of
  the jargon you used to write, rewritten plainly. That's the most powerful
  version of this.
- **Record the six moves as separate clips** so any one can be re-done alone.
- **Callbacks:** tie the "tighter, not shorter" example back to Lesson 2, and at
  the end set up Lesson 5 (Images & Graphics) — once the words are clear, the
  pictures carry the rest. And reinforce that this whole pass happens *before*
  Review (Lesson 10), so your human reviewer's time is spent well.
